How to choose a lifting belts

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The right lifting belt supports your core during heavy squats, deadlifts, and overhead presses by providing a firm wall to brace against. Choose a belt based on your discipline, thick, stiff leather for powerlifting; tapered or thinner for Olympic weightlifting and general training, and prioritise a comfortable, snug fit that stays put without shifting.

What type of lifting belt should I get?

Broadly speaking, lifting belts fall into three camps: powerlifting belts, weightlifting belts, and velcro or nylon belts. Powerlifting belts are thick, uniformly wide (usually 4 inches), and made from stiff leather or suede, think of models from Rogue, SBD, Inzer, or Pioneer. These belts provide a solid wall to push your belly against during maximal squats and deadlifts. Weightlifting belts are typically tapered (wider in the back, narrower in the front) and made from leather or suede that is slightly more flexible. They allow better range of motion for Olympic lifts like cleans and snatches. Velcro belts are the lightest and most adjustable, ideal for CrossFit or circuit training where you need to quickly take the belt on and off, but they don’t offer the same rigid support for one-rep max attempts.

How wide and thick should a lifting belt be?

For powerlifting, the standard width is 4 inches all around, with a thickness of 10 to 13 millimeters, thick enough to transmit force evenly across your midsection. Many competition rules (like the IPF) also cap width at 4 inches. For Olympic lifting or general strength training, a 3-inch wide belt or a tapered 4-inch belt is more common because it doesn’t dig into the ribs during overhead movements. Thinner belts, around 4 to 6 millimeters in suede, are often more comfortable for longer training sessions but provide less rigid bracing. Your torso length also matters: if you have a short torso, a 3-inch belt may fit better without interfering with your ribcage or hip bones.

What is the difference between a prong, lever, and velcro closure?

Prong closures are the classic single- or double-prong design that uses holes to adjust tension. They allow fine-tuning between sets but can be slow to put on and take off. Double prongs are generally sturdier for very heavy lifts, while single prongs are easier to adjust. Lever belts use a quick-release cam mechanism that provides consistent tension every time, you don’t have to fiddle with holes. Brands like SBD and Inzer make popular lever models. The trade-off is that you cannot micro-adjust during a session; you set it once and leave it. Velcro closures are fast and flexible, but they can slip under extreme loads (over 500 pounds), making them best for dynamic or moderate lifting.

How do I size and fit a lifting belt?

Measure your waist at the level where you typically wear a belt, usually right at the navel or slightly above for squats, and lower for deadlifts. Wrap a tape measure snugly and note the measurement in inches or centimeters. Most belt manufacturers provide a sizing chart linking waist circumference to belt size (like small, medium, large). The belt should fit on the tightest notch or one notch in when you first use it, so it can accommodate future break-in or changes in your waist size. A properly fitted belt is snug enough that you can brace your core into it without it moving, but not so tight that you cannot take a full belly breath. You should be able to slide a hand between the belt and your belly only with moderate effort.

Which belt material is best for heavy lifting?

For maximum rigidity under heavy loads, stiff single- or double-ply leather is the gold standard. It requires a break-in period (often weeks of use or rolling the belt) but then molds to your body and offers consistent support. Suede belts are softer and more comfortable right out of the box but may not provide quite as solid a brace for 500-plus pound deadlifts. Nylon or velcro belts are the least supportive for maximal work but are fine for moderate weights and allow unrestricted movement. Some top brands now offer hybrid belts with a stiff core and a suede outer layer, giving you the best of both worlds, but they come at a higher price.

Frequently asked questions

Should I buy a 3-inch or 4-inch belt?

A 4-inch belt is standard for powerlifting because it gives more surface area to brace against. However, if you have a short torso or do a lot of Olympic lifts, a 3-inch or tapered belt often fits better and doesn’t poke into your ribs or hips.

How tight should a lifting belt be?

Tight enough that you can still take a full breath into your belly without the belt loosening, but not so tight that it restricts breathing or causes pain. You should be able to slide a hand between belt and belly only with effort.

Is a lever belt better than a prong belt?

It depends on preference. Lever belts offer consistent tension and are quick to put on, but they lack the micro-adjustment of a prong belt. Many competitive powerlifters prefer lever belts for their uniformity, while prong belts are easier to adjust between exercises.

Can I use a weightlifting belt for deadlifts?

Yes, but a thicker, stiffer belt is generally better for deadlifts to provide a solid brace point. Tapered belts can work, but the front of the belt may not offer as much support as a uniform-width belt.

Do I need a belt for CrossFit or general fitness?

Not necessarily. Velcro belts are common for WODs because they are easy to take on and off and allow movement. If you are lifting heavy singles or squats, a leather belt may be overkill; a nylon velcro belt is sufficient.

In shortThe best lifting belt is one that fits your body, matches your lifting style, and feels comfortable under load. Take the time to measure your waist, consider your main lifts, and break in a quality belt, it will serve you for years.
